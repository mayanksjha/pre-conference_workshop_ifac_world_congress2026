Prognostics, Health Aware Control Design and Safe Control Learning for Safety Critical Systems

A full-day workshop on health-aware control, prognostics-aware control, safe control design and learning, and RUL-oriented control for safety-critical systems.

The programme connects PHM, feedback control, and safe / continual learning through seven invited talks and a closing panel, with applications spanning electric aircraft propulsion, degrading mechatronic systems, batteries, reusable rocket engines, renewable energy systems, and lifelong control.

Abstract

Workshop abstract

This full-day workshop targets taking participants from introductory concepts to recent research frontiers in health-aware control, prognostics-aware control, safe control design and learning, and RUL-oriented control. These topics are particularly challenging because they require combining predictions from Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) modules with feedback control loops in a principled way, while preserving safety, performance, and reliability.

Across seven invited talks, the workshop will first cover model-based and hybrid, physics-informed approaches for fault detection, health assessment, and predictive maintenance of complex systems, including electric aircraft propulsion. Building on these modeling and learning tools, the workshop will present Health-Aware Control (HAC) methodologies that explicitly embed Remaining Useful Life (RUL) in state-space control design, enabling regulation of degradation trajectories and lifetime-oriented decision-making in mechatronic, rocket propulsion, battery, and renewable energy systems. Case studies will illustrate how ageing-aware prediction architectures, such as advanced deep-learning models, can be coupled with RL-based mission planning and set-point modulation strategies to achieve health-aware operation in realistic scenarios.

Then, the workshop will first introduce the foundations of safe and continual reinforcement learning (RL) for control, showing how control-theoretic tools such as Lyapunov and barrier functions can provide formal guarantees on safety and constraint satisfaction, even under non-stationary conditions induced by degradation and faults.

Beyond providing a structured and accessible introduction, the workshop aims to systematically expose open problems and emerging research questions at the intersection of PHM, control, and learning. It targets students, researchers, and practitioners who seek both a clear conceptual entry point and a critical view of current challenges in deploying safe, prognostics- and health-aware autonomy.

Main objectives

  • Unify methodologies: Present a coherent picture of safe, fault-resilient, and health-aware control design where PHM outputs, RUL estimates, and safety constraints are jointly considered in controller and policy design.
  • Advance safe and continual learning in control: Disseminate recent advances in Safe RL and continual RL, emphasizing how formal safety guarantees and lifelong adaptation can be realized in practice for complex systems.
  • Strengthen cross-community collaboration: Connect researchers from IFAC Technical Committees related to fault diagnosis/FTC, sustainable energy systems, optimal control, and hybrid systems, and from the PHM and ML communities, to identify common challenges and shared benchmarks.
  • Identify open problems and research directions: Through the closing panel and discussions, highlight gaps in theory, computation, and practice (e.g., certification and validation) to guide future collaborative efforts.

Scope

Workshop outline and technical scope

The day is structured around three connected themes: model-based and hybrid PHM, application-driven health-aware control, and safe / continual reinforcement learning for non-stationary control settings.

Block 1

Model-based and hybrid PHM

Physics-based and deep-learning hybrid models for fault detection, health estimation, predictive maintenance, and RUL-aware control of degrading mechatronic systems.

Block 2

Application-driven health-aware operation

Battery discharge prediction, health-aware mission planning, prognostics-aware rocket-engine control, and PHM-informed HAC for renewable-energy systems.

Block 3

Safe and continual learning

Safe reinforcement learning with control-theoretic guarantees, safe exploration, input-to-state safety, and continual reinforcement learning for lifelong control.

Confirmed technical topics

Safe RL and safety-critical control design

Control barrier functions, reward shaping, safe exploration, and formal guarantees for nonlinear systems.

Continual and lifelong RL for control

Regularization, replay, and expansion methods for non-stationary environments without catastrophic forgetting.

Hybrid physics-informed PHM

H-PINNs and related hybrid models for health prediction in electric aircraft propulsion and other complex systems.

RUL-driven health-aware control

Observer and controller design that shape degradation rates and end-of-life trajectories.

Health-aware learning and mission planning

Battery ageing estimation, health-aware mission optimization, and operational parameter control.

Prognostics-aware control design

Set-point modulation and optimization strategies that explicitly trade off performance against long-term health.

Renewable-energy HAC

Integration of health and RUL estimates into control loops for reliability-sensitive energy assets.

Invited programme

Talks and abstracts

The workshop comprises seven invited 60-minute talks followed by a closing discussion.

Talk 1

Model Based Approaches for Fault Detection, Prognostics, Decision Making in Complex Systems

Chetan S. Kulkarni (Senior Researcher, KBR / NASA Ames Research Center)

PHMH-PINNseVTOLCondition-based maintenance
View abstract

The airspace is about to be revolutionized by a silent swarm of electric aircraft vehicles. This is the future of aeronautics, with electric Vertical Take-Off and Landing (eVTOL) vehicles buzzing through the low-altitude airspace for both commercial deliveries and passenger transportation. To ensure the safety of this busy airspace, incredibly precise systems for health management will be crucial. These systems won’t just passively monitor the health of the aircraft, but actively predict potential failures before they happen. This will be essential not only to maintain or even surpass current aviation safety standards, but also to optimize maintenance schedules and pave the way for truly autonomous decision-making by the UAMs themselves. With these advanced health management systems, the entire urban air mobility network will be more resilient to unexpected disruptions, keeping the airspace safe and efficient.

The key to solving the prediction problem lies in understanding the aircraft’s current health status in real-time. This allows us to shift from fixed maintenance schedules to “condition-based” predictions, where maintenance is performed when truly necessary based on the vehicle’s health status. To accurately predict the future health of any complex system, we need two things: a clear picture of its current health condition and a good understanding of how it will be used in the future (flight patterns, weather conditions, etc.).

This research proposes a new framework for predicting the health of complex systems, especially those critical for safety, like electric aircraft propulsion systems. It combines two powerful tools:

  • Physics-based performance models: digital representations of the system that capture the fundamental physical laws governing its behavior.
  • Deep learning algorithms: methods that excel at uncovering hidden patterns in large datasets.

The physics-based model can be seen as a blueprint that may have missing modeling pieces or uncertainties. Deep learning, using real-world sensor data, fills these gaps and identifies subtle patterns to learn degrading parameters that might indicate potential problems. This hybrid approach using H-PINNs is particularly well-suited for the challenges faced by electric aircraft propulsion systems, offering accurate and adaptable models for predicting health and paving the way for safer, more efficient, and more reliable electric aircraft.

Talk 2

Health-Aware Control for Mechatronic Systems in Degradation

John-Jairo Martinez-Molina (Professor, Grenoble INP, France)

RULMechatronicsObserversRobust control
View abstract

Remaining Useful Life (RUL) has become widely recognized as a key metric for evaluating system reliability within the field of Prognostics and Health Management (PHM). It plays a crucial role in predicting a system’s state of health and its expected lifespan. While significant efforts have been directed towards accurately estimating RUL, the process of decision-making regarding health management and potential adjustments to equipment lifespans still raises questions. This talk introduces a state-space-based methodology designed to effectively manage RUL and, thus, the end-of-life of a degrading system, particularly focusing on controlled mechatronics systems. Through an investigation of the relationship between the operational dynamics of the system and its aging process, the talk specifically concentrates on regulating the degradation rate experienced during the system operation. A robust design method of an observer and a controller is presented to effectively govern the degradation trajectory, allowing for the achievement of an acceptable level of deterioration within a desired average lifetime. Several practical examples will be presented to illustrate the control and the estimation methodology. The results highlight the benefits of using RUL control as a Health-Aware Control (HAC) strategy, aiming to find an optimal balance between reliability and production objectives.

Talk 3

From Ageing-Aware Battery Discharge Prediction to Optimal Health-Aware Operation

Olga Fink (Professor, EPFL, Switzerland)

BatteriesDynaformerHealth-aware mission planningDeep RL
View abstract

In this talk, we will introduce Dynaformer, a novel deep learning architecture designed to infer the ageing state from limited voltage/current samples and accurately predict the full voltage discharge curve. Our evaluation demonstrates that Dynaformer performs well on simulated data and, with minimal fine-tuning, effectively bridges the gap to real-world battery data. This methodology enables controlled and predictable battery usage until end-of-discharge, extending operating cycles and reducing costs.

Building upon these health-aware discharge predictions, we will address the joint problem of mission planning and health-aware real-time control of operational parameters. We will present a deep reinforcement learning algorithm that proactively prescribes operational parameters to extend the discharge cycle based on the battery's current health status while optimizing the mission. Evaluation using simulated flights of a NASA conceptual multirotor aircraft model, gathered from hardware-in-the-loop experiments, demonstrates the algorithm’s near-optimal performance across various operational scenarios, allowing adaptation to changing environmental conditions.

Talk 4

Prognostics-Aware Control Design for Extended Remaining Useful Life: Application to Liquid Propellant Reusable Rocket Engine

Didier Theilliol and Mayank Shekhar Jha (CRAN, Université de Lorraine, France)

Prognostics-aware controlRUL extensionRocket enginesSet-point modulation
View abstract

As most of the safety critical industrial systems remain sensitive to functional degradation and operate under closed loop, it becomes imperative to take into account the state of health within the control design process. To that end, an effective assessment and extension of the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of complex systems is a standing challenge that seeks novel solutions at the cross-over of Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) domain as well as automatic control. This paper considers a dynamical system subjected to functional degradation and presents a novel control design strategy, wherein the assessment of state of health of the system is taken into account leading to effective prediction of the RUL as well as its extension. To that end, the degradation model is considered unknown but input-dependent. The control design is formulated as an optimization problem wherein a suitable compromise is reached between the performance and desired RUL of the system. The main contribution of the paper remains in proposal of a set-point modulation-based approach wherein the control input at a given present time stage is modulated in such way that futuristic health of the system over a long time horizon is extended whilst assuring acceptable performance. The effectiveness of the proposed strategy is assessed in simulation using a numerical example as well as a liquid propellant rocket engine case.

Talk 5

Health-Aware Control of Renewable Energy Systems

Vicenç Puig (Professor, Universitat Politècnica de Catalunya, Spain)

Renewable energyHACPHM integrationReliability
View abstract

The research on the control of renewable energy systems has been mainly focused on the performance optimization without caring about the system degradation. Health-aware control (HAC) is an advanced control strategy that integrates the health status of system components into the control system. It aims to optimize system performance while considering the degradation and remaining useful life (RUL) of critical components. By using the information coming from a prognostics and health management (PHM) module, HAC adjusts control actions to extend the lifespan of components and prevent failures. HAC is particularly valuable in industries where system reliability and longevity are crucial, such as aerospace, automotive, and manufacturing. This talk aims at bringing together cutting-edge research and innovative methodologies aimed at enhancing system reliability and performance, featuring approaches to HAC, PHM, and their integration, so as to obtain a control system that optimizes maintenance, predicts failures, and extends the lifespan of critical components. The resulting frameworks are valuable for researchers, engineers, and practitioners dedicated to improving system health and operational efficiency in renewable energy applications.

Talk 6

Safe Reinforcement Learning with Guarantees - Towards Safe and Health-Aware Control? Some Recent Advances for Continuous-Time and Discrete-Time Nonlinear Systems

Mayank Shekhar Jha (Associate Professor, CRAN, Université de Lorraine, France)

Safe RLCBFsISS / ISSfNonlinear systems
View abstract

This talk presents recent advances in Safe Reinforcement Learning (Safe RL) methodologies applicable to both discrete-time and continuous-time systems with provable guarantees. The talk will introduce reinforcement learning (Adaptive Dynamic Programming) and motivate Safe RL. Then, Safe RL approaches developed for discrete-time systems will be presented where safety is enforced by augmenting CBFs into the reward structure, thereby ensuring the forward invariance of predefined safe sets. Further, the Safe Exploration problem will be elaborated, highlighting how Input-to-State Stability (ISS) properties can be exploited to maintain safety during the exploration phase. In this context, the concept of Input-to-State Safety (ISSf) is introduced, offering a novel framework that promotes rich, risk-aware exploration while preserving formal safety guarantees. The talk further covers recent developments addressing input saturation constraints, strategies for boundary-focused exploration, and model-free methods for system dynamics learning, contributing to a more robust and generalizable Safe RL framework.

Talk 7

Continual Reinforcement Learning Control for Lifelong Control

Austin Coursey, Marcos Quiñones-Grueiro, and Gautam Biswas (Vanderbilt University)

Continual RLLifelong controlNon-stationaritySafety constraints
View abstract

Accurate models are often unavailable for in-depth analysis of complex engineering systems, making data-driven methods such as reinforcement learning (RL) attractive for decision-making and control. While modern RL can learn flexible nonlinear policies from data, it commonly assumes stationary environments. In contrast, real-world systems change over time due to shifting environmental conditions, component degradation, faults, and evolving objectives. This talk surveys Continual (Lifelong) Reinforcement Learning, which aims to enable long-term adaptation for control of systems that undergo multiple non-stationary changes without catastrophic forgetting. We provide a formal representation of continual RL agents, explain their relevance to control, and distinguish them from related paradigms, including multi-task RL and meta-RL. We then discuss three core classes of continual RL methods: regularization-based approaches, experience-replay strategies, and model or policy expansion techniques, and highlight recent case studies that illustrate their strengths and limitations. We conclude with key challenges and open problems, with emphasis on maintaining safety constraints during lifelong adaptation.
